# Fundamentals Cutoffs

The [prioritisation survey]({{ site.baseurl }}/prioritisation-survey/) flags five upstream areas if you fall below a particular threshold. This page lists those thresholds and the evidence behind them.

The thresholds mark where things start to noticeably go wrong, which is usually well short of the recommended optimum. Where validated short questionnaires exist (PHQ-2, GAD-2, WHO-5, UCLA-3), the survey uses those.

## Sleep

**Flag if:** under 6 hours per night on typical work nights.

The 7–9 hour range is the standard recommendation, but most of the harm from falling short of it kicks in below 6 hours. A meta-analysis of 1.4 million people (Cappuccio et al., 2010) found that mortality rises about 12% once you drop below that line.

## Mental wellbeing

**Flag if:** low score on the WHO-5 wellbeing index (≤ 13 out of 25).

The triage stage already screens for clinical-level depression and anxiety. WHO-5 catches a different group: people whose wellbeing is interfering with daily life without rising to clinical depression or anxiety. ([Topp et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25831962/) systematic review.)

## Foundational finances

**Flag if:** less than $1,000 in savings, or balances above 15% interest on credit cards or similar debt.

[Mani et al. (2013)](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1238041) found that financial strain takes up roughly the cognitive bandwidth of losing 13 IQ points. Recommending career or productivity changes to someone in that state tends to fall flat.

Mortgages and student loans aren't included. Their harm curves are much flatter than revolving consumer credit.

## Connection

**Flag if:** high score on the UCLA-3 loneliness scale, or less than weekly meaningful contact with close friends or family.

[Holt-Lunstad et al. (2015)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1745691614568352), pooling 3.4 million people, found social isolation carries roughly the same mortality risk as smoking 15 cigarettes a day.

Loneliness and frequency of contact are partly independent, so the survey flags either.

## Physical activity and nutrition

**Flag if:** under an hour a week of moderate-to-vigorous activity, or fewer than 3 daily servings of vegetables or fruit.

Most national guidelines suggest 150 minutes a week of activity. The largest health gain, though, comes from moving from nothing to something – the jump from 0 to 60 minutes is much bigger than from 150 to 300. The threshold targets the inactive end.

For nutrition, the threshold focuses on a single well-supported signal. Broader questions about what you eat (sugar, processed food, fibre) live in the [Nutrition life area]({{ site.baseurl }}/nutrition/).

---

These thresholds are stated more precisely than the evidence really supports. Harm curves are continuous; lines have to be drawn somewhere. Expect them to move as the survey accumulates responses.
